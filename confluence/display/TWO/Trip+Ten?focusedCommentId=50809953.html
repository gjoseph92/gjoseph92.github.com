    Not for beginners!  Even through hikers were doing the same distances as we were on this strenuous section.

    For weather reasons, the two years before us the ended up staying two nights at FULL GOOSE, as South Peak is not actually a shelter, but just a peak. The pick up is just as easy, as you come out just a little further up on the same logging road.  In fact, we walked down to the closer pick up, so the bus wouldn't have to go as far down the dirt road. South Peak is a mere 0.3 mi after Full Goose shelter I believe. Anyway, less bad ass trips beware this trip contains the "hardest mile on the AT" going through the Mahoosuc Notch, a 1.5 mile bolder field that took 3 hours to cross, on day four. Despite, its difficulty, I think it was everyone's favorite part of the trip.  But my group had a fair amount of experience.

    Most of the shelters have bear boxes but the rodents are terrible.  One night we had Adam chasing them out of the shetler, and another I woke up with a mouse on my eye.  Every night, I believe we had an outhouse too.

    Hey boys and girls.

    This is damn hard. A few things while they're fresh in my mind.

    The trailhead isn't really hard to find but you need to turn on a road to get across the river (I think it's "Meadow Road"). You're looking for a white fence with a turnstile (sounds weird, but it's actually a turnstile) and then you're on the trail. Day 1 isn't hard but be sure to have an AMC Guide to find your way around the logging roads to get to camp, which has a gorgeous view and a not-so-gorgeous lake. Also, the side hike to the waterfall is well worth it.

    After Day 1 it gets wild, Days 2 and 3 were really really tough. Like Laura said, they're not really long but you do cover some extremely technical hiking. It's great for on-trail team-building (pack passing, etc) but don't expect to get into camp early. In fact, A2 left their camp every morning by 830 and never got into camp (about 4 or 5 miles away) before 5 or so, with less than an hour until sundown. This is a bummer for teaching bear-bagging, tarp setup, cooking, etc. We were talking to some through-hikers and telling them that we were doing a pre-orientation with some greenhorns and they said "Why the hell would you choose this section?" So there.

    Also, we took Day 4 as a lay-over (due to weather and general fatigue). This is really not that big of a deal and you can wait until you're on the trail to decide, the pickup is easier to get to from Full Goose than the farther site anyway.

    Finally, the pickup is sort of hard to find. You pop out on an older logging road, walk down for a bit, follow the signs, then pop out on a road where a turn-around would seem to be. THIS IS NOT THE END. Turn LEFT and go for another few hundred yards to the Success Pond Road for your pickup. If you start going up a hill and the creek's on your right, you've gone the wrong direction.

    Boomshakalaka. A2 Phone Home.

    NH10: Awesome trip over all, but be prepared to be a cheerleader for your kids, they'll need it.

    This trip was REALLY challenging and we knew this going in so we can't really complain; The fact that 2009 was the Year of the Hurricane for TWO didn't make it any easier. I've polled our kids though, and most seemed to say that the difficulty was bonding which is sweet.

    Some notes:
    -We didn't have any problems drop-off/pickup. The Drop-Off is literally a wooden fence and turnstyle like somebody stole it from the Merry-go-Round from Coney Island in the 1920s.
    -I'm the sure the Ridge on Days 2 and 3 would normally have been beautiful but instead it was white-out due to snow/ice. Be careful with the wood-planks which go through wet-lands on the top of the ridge. They are mad slippery when wet and easy for someone to fall off of. Everybody likes getting muddy but there were a few broken bone close calls.
    -A17 successfully pushed straight through Mahoosic Notch (rather than take a layover-day like Hoopes did, see above)because the weather cleared up on the 4th day. It was good for pack-passing and teambuilding but nerve racking because it is hard to keep all of your kids in your sight at once. If it IS raining and your kids ARE NOT 100% on their game, I would recommend doing a layover day. Doing the Notch in the rain would be dangerous.
    -THE END OF THE BOULDER FIELD IS NOT THE END OF THE DAY! When you get out of the Notch, there is at least another 2 hours of hiking because you go straight up a mountain for a while, and once at the top have to follow the ridge to the campsite at Speck Pond. We didn't realize this and got into camp later than we wanted for the pizza making extravagenza on night 4.

    That's about all. Rebecca Graffy and I had an awesome time, and don't regret picking a harder trip.
    But maybe thats because we were THE BREAKFAST REVOLUTION. A17 through and through, baby.

    From Anya Price, TWO 2010

    In 2010 I led a trip with Brian Rowe (A4).  I forget which number it
    was, but we started in New Hampshire and ended in Maine, going on the
    Mahoosuc Trail and the AT.  We supposedly had the "hardest mile of the
    AT" in Mahoosuc Notch (although many trips seem to claim this).  We
    hit Mt Success one day, which is very exposed, but it's a great
    meet-up point with other groups/Support Staff hike-ins.

    Overall, this trip was a great backpacking one.  There were some
    challenging moments, such as steep cliffs where we would have to
    pack-pass, and we had a couple longer days.  The reason it was so
    great was because, as you may recall, the weather was PERFECT.  We had
    perfect sunshine every day, which was why we decided to go for the
    Mahoosuc Notch.  There is an alternative that BRowe and I planned for
    Day 4, in which we could stay at our campsite and do a day trip (but
    we didn't ultimately need to use it).  The Notch is known to get very
    dangerous when it's rainy, so it's a good idea to have a back-up plan,
    and we were lucky enough to have multiple evac routes that we didn't
    need.

    Whoever chooses to do this trip should get their kids psyched about it
    early on.  It's definitely one of the more challenging trips.  We
    didn't have the bonding experiences of surviving Irene, but getting
    through the Mahoosuc Notch was a big morale boost, and the freshmen
    were excited about completing it after we hyped it up for them.  Be
    prepared to go slowly, but with great weather we were able to make it
    to camp by 6 pm every day, which was awesome.  There weren't many
    thru-hikers so we got to stay at shelters a couple nights.

    If you choose this trip, be prepared: NH10 is for rockstars only. The trip isn't just physically demanding but it includes some very technical hiking as well. You'll need to keep your freshmen motivated throughout as there are no easy days and the hardest day, the Mahoosuc, comes near the end. But if you're up for the challenge and think that your freshmen can handle it, it's worth it: you get a ton of peaks, the views are unbelievable, and there's no doubt your group will bond over the challenges.

    In terms of campsites, there were shelters and outhouses every night and bearboxes every site except for site 3. Our trail had a lot of thruhikers, so we only spent nights 2 and 3 in the shelters. The campsites all had additional platforms to sleep on that were comfortable, but the trees weren't well-suited for tarps. We made due but just realize that if you don't get to the shelters first you'll be spending more time than usual for tarps.

    We were concerned with water during trip-planning, but it wasn't too much of an issue. Your only reliable water sources on days 2 and 3 are at your campsites, which ended up being enough, and there's also a small brook about halfway through day 2. I wouldn't rely on it but we've had a drought this year and it was still going strong.

    Day 1: The drop-off is simple--like other reviews have said just look for the white fence and turnstile. It's a shorter hike, but a lot of uphill and little shade. You have the option of doing a side trip to the Dryad Falls, which was a nice spot for a break but honestly Sam & I were expecting them to be cooler, although this might be due to this year's drought. Great views from the Gentian Pond campsite and our freshmen loved swimming in the pond, although the water's real muddy.

    Day 2: Lots of uphill to Mt. Success. The peak is pretty exposed but the views are amazing and it's a great spot for lunch. There's still quite a bit of elevation from that point on, so plan accordingly.

    Day 3: You start the day off with significant elevation, but once you get up to Mt. Carlo you'll spend most of the day just hopping along the peaks. The view from Full Goose is really something else and a great way to celebrate what you've already accomplished.

    Day 4: The Mahoosuc Notch is the hardest mile on the AT, and it's earned that title. It's straight boulders and you'll be doing a lot of crawling, jumping, and pack-passing. It took us over 3 hours to complete and we were working hard. We lucked out with weather, but I would have serious reservations about hiking the notch during or immediately after rain. Other groups in the past have done this day as a day hike without packs and gone in and out the notch, but I wouldn't recommend that either: the last thing your group will want to do is hike the notch twice. Your day isn't over after the notch: you've still got quite a bit of hiking left with a lot of elevation. Luckily there's an awesome peak towards the end and Speck Pond is great for swimming.

    Day 5: The hikeout's pretty simple; you start the day with some good elevation for the first mile, but then it's all downhill. You hike alongside the Sucker Brook, so water isn't an issue at all. You'll come out right on Success Pond Rd if you do the trip the whole way through, so you can just wait at the trailhead.

    All in all, this trip is really difficult but I highly recommend it. None of your days are really high mileage, so as long as you plan accordingly you'll never have to worry about getting to camp at night. You also cover seven peaks with unbelievable views, have four great campsites, and get a lot of opportunities to swim. Not to mention at the end your group can say they've done the hardest mile on the whole damn AT.

    A1 lives to poop another day.

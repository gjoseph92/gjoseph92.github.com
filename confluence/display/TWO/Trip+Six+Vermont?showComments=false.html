    First off I don't think this is the right map. It's similar to our trip, but not our map. We went down the Chittendon Brook trail on day 3 (camped down there that night), and then back to the LT the next day. I can't spot where we went out, but it was further down the road. And I can't figure out where we went in exactly, but I have my maps and I can look it up....

    And now for the fun stuff..... There's a good chance that the first night's shelter -- not the skyline lodge, this isn't our map exactly, but I can't figure out what it was called -- does not exist. It's only mentioned in one guide book. And we couldn't find it. That being said because of bus trouble (to put it mildly---it took us three buses to actually get there) we didn't get there until dark and had to night hike right through to our second night's spot. But Perrin couldn't find it either the year before. And Perrin's pretty badass. Therefore I would not recommend running this trip until confirming that the first campsite does indeed exist, and marking it well. If there are no problems, hiking through to the second site isn't really that far and is doable (our kids did it on empty stomachs in pitch black for six hours with lots of rustling in the bushes), but that's really no way to start a trip, so I'd check it out. Our trip ended up great, but I wouldn't wish that first night on anyone in a million years.

    Hmmm we had a whole mess of issues figuring out where this trip was.

    First off, the groups that are listed on here (Liz and Josh and Daniel and El) didn't do this trip. Also our route was a bit different from the one listed above.  

    In general, this is a pretty easy trip.  There is very little elevation.  One pretty swim.  Very few views - because there's so little elevation, you never really make it above tree-line.  One plus about this area of the Long Trail at this time of year is that it's very uncrowded.  We only passed a few other hikers, and often got to sleep in the shelters.  There are also no caretakers.  Water sources are an issue (especially at Boyce).  We very rarely had cell service.  It's pretty straight-forward navigation-wise because you are just going south on the LT the whole time.  When we were there, there was still flood damage from Hurricane Irene on the Chittenden Brook Trail so we skipped it - look into that before you decide to stay there.  Also, Worth Mountain Lodge is closed (and maybe burned down?) so don't plan to stay there.  Keep a look out for the trailhead to Burnt Hill Trail when you're on the bus - we passed it the first time and had to turn around.  It's poorly marked, but there is a sign on the left side of the road.  There's no parking lot there.

    Here was our route:

    1. Hike in on Burnt Hill Trail to Boyce Shelter, where there's a three-wall shelter and campground. Hike up Burnt Hill Trail was steep but short. THERE WAS NO WATER ANYWHERE NEAR THIS CAMPSITE.  It was a particularly dry summer, but apparently water sources are generally sketchy there anyways.  We had been told about a small brooke that crosses the Long Trail a little north of the shelter, however it was dry.  We had to send Dan and 2 freshmen up to Skyline Pond (2 miles north), where there was a nasty bog.  We didn't hit a water source until Day 2 at Pleiad Lake.  

    2. This is a long day.  Hiked south to Sucker Brook shelter.  Lake Pleiad is BEAUTIFUL (maybe you'll run into a lovely 80-yr. old skinny-dipping couple like us), definitely worth a lunch, swim, and water break.  Right before hitting the lake you'll hike down to a highway, cross the highway, and then hike back up to a ski lift.  Unfortunately it's a long day, and you hit the lake pretty early, so make sure not to spend too long there.  We had a little bit of trouble finding the LT going south from Lk. Pleiad so pay better attention than us (it's closer than we thought it was).  Worth Mtn. is not spectacular.  We didn't even notice when we passed the peak.  Water at Sucker Brook is a little ways down Sucker Brook Trail.  It's a relatively large brook, but was pretty dry when we were there.  That said, you can find some reasonable streams to fill up.  Hike up or down the brook a little ways.  There's a small three-wall shelter there, and some tent sites (no platforms).

    3. Hiked to Sunrise Shelter.  Somewhere around the middle of this day, there's a small rock outcropping with a nice view.  Great Cliffs are definitely worth the detour.  It's a super short trail (but mostly up).  Best view we had on the trip!When you hit highway 73 after the Great Cliffs, you have to hike a little east (down) along the highway to get back to the long trail.  Three-walled shelter.  Picnic table.  There's an AMAZING water source 50 feet south on the Long Trail from the shelter.  You can't miss it, there's a big bridge over a river.  

    4. Hiked south to David Logan.  This day is long and flat and boring. It was a good day for solo hikes.  You'll hit a little bridge with a river around lunch time that's nice for lunch and a water fill-up.  David Logan has a cool three-wall shelter with bunks.  There's also tent sites with no platform.  You pass the water source right before you enter the campsite.  It's a small stream, that someone's put a PVC pipe in to channel the water (look for the penis-like object). Take the rubber-band and cloth off and it'll go faster.

    5. Quick, easy, down-hill hike-out.  We hiked out all the way to Mountain Top Inn, because we didn't know if the bus would be able to make it on the road to us (or find us), but it turns out it probably would've fine to wait for the bus at the parking lot at the end of forest road 99.  

    Have fun!!  Feel free to contact us if you have any questions (laurelstarr1@gmail.com or dankatzzeiger@gmail.com).

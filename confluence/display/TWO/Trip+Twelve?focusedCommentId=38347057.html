    This trip is fantastic... if it has been a dry summer and the river is not too high. DEFINITELY CHECK ON THIS! We had to bushwack on one side of the river because it was impossible to cross. Another possibility is to hike in to rocky branch #2 on the first day and then do Mt. Isolation as a day hike on the second day, stay at Rocky Branch #2 again, and then go on to Hermit Lake and follow the trip as planned.
    We did, however, have shelters every night and they were fantastic.
    The fourth day up Washington, again, is great if you have a group that is in good enough shape. There is a lot of rock scrambling and potential for injury, so keeping the group close together is super important. Again, totally worth it. Come down Lions Head instead of Tuckerman, it is steep at the end, but not as long as Tuckerman and definitely a better descent.
    Do this hike, but definitely check out the variations I mentioned above depending on the depth of the river this year.

    2012 Trip Leaders: Eric Archibald and Jenny Smith

    This is a good trip. It’s moderately challenging, though our group was all beginners

    and they did fine. Although there’s no one that stands out in terms of mileage, the

    second day (1st full day) was by far the most challenging and long. There’s a long,

    moderate hike to get to Bootspur, which took us most of the morning. The top of

    Bootspur was definitely the challenge. It’s a long exposed ridgeline that can quite

    easily get windy. We ran into some weather and had to push our freshmen to move

    quickly on the descent, which felt dangerous at times. Going down Bootspur was

    difficult and extremely slow moving. The path is a glorified scree-field, and very

    technically difficult for beginner hikers.

    Washington was a great day. It’s challenging, but leaving most of our gear at the

    campsite was a luxury, and our freshmen felt very accomplished at the top. Hermit

    Lake Shelters are the Ritz Carleton of the wilderness. Compared to Rocky Branch #2

    (where we set up tarps on the ground because the shelter was taken and there were

    no platforms), it’s paradise.

    The fourth day was a nice hike, without much elevation change. This is a good day

    to pair hikes. Make sure when leaving Hermit lake you keep your eyes peeled for

    Raymond path as it is not well marked.

    Osgood tent site is nice, but not a good place to set up tarps. There are no trees close

    to the tent platforms, making it nearly impossible to get the tarps up. We got poured

    on the last night, and the tarps leaked right away. Rough night. Osgood did have very

    nice bear-proof boxes, eliminating the need to bear bag (which would have been

    difficult with the trees available), and had a place to make a fire.

    The hike out was easy, though confusingly marked at times. The Great Gulf trail

    turns into a cross-country ski path. We didn’t really see that on our map, and luckily

    took the right trail to get to the trailhead, which said ‘to D.C’, which we took to

    mean “Dolly Copp” campground.

    Overall, we had no difficulty finding water or places to bear bag. Give yourself a lot

    of time on the Bootspur day.

    --Jenny & Eric

    Ok, so this is a really easy trip. Flat almost the entire time, with some hills every once in a while. The first night, there's no exact campsite, so we just set up in a little clearing right off the trail just before a wooden bridge after the last fork in the road that day, the one where you go left. Also, no place to bear bag, so we just wrapped our stuff up in a ground tarp. We only had room for one tarp, so we all packed in for the night. Water is right there. Oh, about a mile into the hike, there's a trail up to a hut. Take it and play in the waterfall/cascade for a while before continuing on. It's very much worth it and a great spot for lunch, especially the first one.

    Second night, you camp at the foot of mount desolation. On the hike there, there was a creek crossing to find the trail. It's hard to see, but it's to the left on the other bank. This is a fairly large campsite with a few spots to set up your tarps. We set up two and let the freshmen sleep alone in one that night. Great spot for a quick cold bath in the creek. We were supposed to hike desolation and stay another night, but we were concerned that some of our freshmen couldn't do it, so we just hiked to the Sawyer Pond shelter a day early.

    The hike was easy, you just go through one short pass and it's mostly flat. We did solos here. We spent a lot of time playing, talking and swimming in the pond that day. One of the freshmen led us in yoga exercises and meditation, etc. And we met Yale FOOT and some old people. The next day was spent swimming and getting to know each other, just keeping busy. We did iron chef to finish our food and did a short solo period to get everyone some alone time and to buy us time to make awards.

    We had to get to the bus stop by 8 am, so we got up really early because it was a 4 mile hike. We saw a moose in the distance on the way and made really good time. We got to the bus stop as the bus rolled in, a full hour early or so. When you get to the creek crossing, you're about 20 feet from the parking lot where the bus picks you up.

    Summary: Easy hike, water sources the entire time, you will always be closer than you think to your destinations. Lots of time to get to know each other and lots of flexibility in terms of how much you hike each day, which will always be fairly small.

    Katie Reiser says:

    -Lots of swimming! Definitely go to Zealand Hut on Day 1, Thor...

    -Lots of swimming! Definitely go to Zealand Hut on Day 1, Thoreau Falls, various spots along the trail, and Sawyer Pond to die for... we even found a natural water slide on Day 2.

    -Not much elevation except for Mt. Carrigan on Day 3. Our group skipped the day hike because of an evacuation but supposedly the view from the peak is the best in the area.

    -Campsites not so great at Thoreau Falls and Desolation Trailhead. Like Dave said, hard to bearbag and you just pull off the side of the trail to set up tarps. Awesome Sawyer Pond campsite makes up for it though- guaranteed tent platforms and/or shelter, fire pits, opportunity to streak groups from Yale, etc.

    -FLOODING / TRAIL DAMAGE. We had a lot of trouble staying on the trail Day 2 due to flooding, mud, downed trees, and overgrowth. Possibly it will be maintained better next year, but it was really tough going and we relied on the compass once or twice.

    -EVAC ROUTE needs to be changed. We had a non-emergent evac on Day 3 and followed the route highlighted on the map... turns out it is much easier to follow the planned trail to the parking lot near Sawyer Pond Road!

    -You can meet up with NH Trip 4 if you rig it right on the morning of Day 2.

    -Overall the waterfalls and pond make this trip great. Easy hiking (although the day hike is supposed to be challenging) if the trails are well-kept.

    Previous comments do a good job recapping this trip. I'll just hit some things to consider for next year.

     Day 1 - Had to do a bit of scouting to find a campsite. We found a spot between the junction where a trail leads to Thoreau Falls and the footbridge.

    Day 2 - Wet and muddy and slippery, though it rained this entire day. IMPORTANT: There is no legitimate camping at the Desolation Trailhead, at least not that we found. So we camped illegitmately. Future coordinators and trip leaders need to figure out an alternative, otherwise there are only sites which have no camping signs at them...

    Day 3 - Several options--we thought about a day hike which would have summitted Mt. Carrigain via the Desolation Trail and continued along the Signal Ridge Trail. We opted not to due to weather and hustled to Sawyer Pond Shelter via the Carrigain Notch Trail instead. There is camping and water available at the junction of Signal Ridge Trail and Carrigain Notch Trail should you pursue the dayhike. Thanks to the group of sketchy alums who scouted that for us.

    Day 4 - Chilled at Sawyer Pond all day. Watch out for bees, Emma and I stepped on a hive a few hundred feet from the shelter along the bank of the pond and came away with four or five stings apiece.

     Day 5 - Wake up early and move at a good pace and the final five miles out or so can be done in about three hours. Maybe a little less. Make sure you go beyond the dirt parking loat and road and out to the paved Kancamagus Highway so the bus sees you.

     Overall great trip, the flatness was fine considering the poor weather we had night one through day two. Feel free to contact me with any questions!

    Day 1- again no real campsite, we opted to find a random place to camp in the middle of the woods- probably not best option. I would definitely suggest coming up with a different plan for campsite the first night- maybe plan to sleep at Zealand hut? The falls are awesome though.

    Day 2- there is a great place to camp if you keep going past the  illegal camp site on your right, look for a blocked path- there were two small trees or branches blocking the entrance for us on the left- perfect campsite, tons of room- Elmore had a hand drawn map that was accurate to the foot. We stayed there two nights, formed a "party tarp" with two tarps overlapping and everyone underneath comfortably. Great swimming here too, although very cold.

    Day 3- day hike up desolation trail. very steep at some places, would be very difficult in bad weather. we did it and everyone was fine, there is an observation tower on top of Mt. Carrigan that is really cool. Elevation still isnt massive, as you dont really end up above tree line.

    Day 4- Sawyer pond is amazing- definitely try to get there early so people can swim. If you go to the far campsite there is a shelter that looks right out over the pond with the mountains in the background- really beautiful. Hike out in the morning is pretty easy,took only a few hour. You need to go up the dirt road to the highway to get picked up.

    We did have a lot  evidence of bears on our trip, so an animal safety training session would be greatly appreciated.  also right before reaching the parking lot requires walking through a river/ stream.

    2012 Leaders: John Kwon and Linzy Prudden

    Difficulty of hiking: Easy. Flat the whole time with an optional day hike that includes a peak
    State of Trails: Okay. It seemed to me like they hadn't been hiked/maintained in a while. Some were relatively muddy. Still doable, though. 
    No shelters except on the last night, but the only shelter on the last night is a small lean-to. Unless the group is the first to arrive, the lean-to is likely to already be occupied (this happened to us). The last night has platforms as well, but there were no good tarp tying trees. We were forced to sleep in the outhouse to stay dry. 
    Water sources were available the entire time.
    If the weather is supposed to be bad, prepare as much as you can and bring waterPROOF (not just water resistant) tarps if possible. If it had rained the entire trip, I'm pretty sure we would have been wet and miserable the whole time. Since it only rained the last day for us it wasn't a huge deal (besides the fact that our group split up between the lean to and the outhouse to stay dry), but if it had rained for more of the trip it would have been difficult, I think.
    --Linzy

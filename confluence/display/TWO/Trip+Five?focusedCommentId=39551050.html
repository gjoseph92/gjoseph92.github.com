    This trip is fairly straightforward in terms of trailheads, forks, etc. Day one is a solid uphill hike. Day two make sure you bring plenty of extra water. We ran out at about lunchtime, having only two nalgenes apiece. I would recommend leaders filling up a few extra water bladders and/or nalgenes each. Besides that, if you get good weather (like we did) the views are amazing. If the weather is questionable, know that the ridge is entirely exposed, and this is what you will be hiking on for most of the day. Day three is nice and easy. When you arrive at the Franconia area, you will go over a river on a footbridge. After the footbridge, make an almost immediate right to go down to the river bank. There is a trail down there which leads to the campsite. You WILL have to ford the river twice after crossing the bridge unless you want to tack on a couple extra miles to go around. Day four is awesome, have fun at Franconia Falls. A little ways up the river is a rock that has been safe to jump from the two times I've been there. Day five, easy flat hike out to the Visitor Center.

    Melissa A. Reifers says:

    AWESOME TRIP!! Definitely challenging, but the hiking is totally worth it for th...

    AWESOME TRIP!! Definitely challenging, but the hiking is totally worth it for the amazing views off of the ridge on the second day. Bonus of doing a trip in New Hampshire is a really short bus ride, which is great for this trip because the hiking on the first day is uphill and not the easiest. The last 0.2 miles of the day is extremely steep downhill into the campsite (Garfield Ridge)- make sure that you go slow, if the rocks are slippery/wet it would be easy to fall and twist an ankle.

    Garfield Ridge is a great campsite, big platforms (try to get there earlier in the day so that you can be sure to grab a platform that is big enough for everyone + gear) and an outhouse. Day 2 requires you to hike straight up the difficult 0.2 mile downhill that ended day 1, and then you continue on to summit Lafayette, Lincoln and Little Haystack. There is no water source on this day so make sure to conserve your water and fill up at Garfield before you leave (Matt brought an extra water bladder and I gave most of my water to freshmen and we were fine). The hike up to Lafayette is the most challenging of the day; there are a couple false summits on the way up and a lot of it is scrambling over bare rock. This is the day to be really conscious of the weather, as you'll be completely exposed on the ridge for the entire day.

    The hike down into Liberty Springs Campsite is also a steep downhill section, but the campsite is great (water source, outhouse, platforms). Note: For both Garfield and Liberty Springs the bear bagging trees are pretty bad, but the caretakers said that the main concern was mice/rodents, so just double-wrap your food bags in tarps and then try to tie them up and that should help.

    The beginning of Day 3 is some uphill up to Mt. Liberty and Mt. Flume and then the rest is nice and easy downhill (good section for pair hiking). Note: The evac routes that are currently highlighted for day 3 (Liberty Spring/Flume Slide) are extremely steep and rocky, so we would suggest that if you need an evac on this day, it might be faster and safer to just continue down Osseo Trail and get picked up at the Visitor Center.

    When you get down to the Franconia area, you'll parallel the river for a bit and then cross the river on a wooden footbridge- immediately after crossing the bridge you go take a right immediately down a trail to the river bank. You follow this trail (it will cross an offshoot of the river, but make sure to keep going straight across to where the trail picks up on the other side), and then it leads to where you ford the main river and you can see the campground on the other side. Day 4 is awesome, have fun hanging out and swimming at the falls all day. There are two water slides that are great, and then farther up the river there is a rock that is safe to jump from. Night 4 will usually have three groups at the Franconia Brook East Campsite so it's a great night for a support staff hike-in. Day 5 is a ridiculously easy flat walk out to the Visitor Center.

      This pretty much says it all from what I can remember! Also, concerning Flume Slide as an evac route, I don't know for sure but a bunch of hikers along the way told us that it's extremely difficult going downhill - and maybe even impossible in the rain - especially considering it would be used as an evac, potentially with a big pack on.

It can be a little confusing coming from the way you come during the trip, and at the very least it's a nice walk to spend with your co-leader! I wouldn't say to do it if it didn't take us less than 1.5 hours to go and come back (I think? Maybe a little longer? Depending on how fast you walk?)

      Overall, like Melissa said, AMAZING trip!

    2012 Leaders: Alex Flores and Emily Wyner

    NH-5 is a challenging trip, particularly in toward the beginning. Day 1, you get off the bus and have a solid 4.8 miles uphill (almost to the peak of Garfield), followed by a steep .2 miles down to camp. Day 2 is by far the hardest day: you start with that steep, scramble-y .2 miles up that you climbed down the day before, and go on to summit Garfield, Lafayette, Lincoln, and Little Haystack. We didn't summit Lafayette until around 2:30PM, and we were originally shooting for around noon, so if you noticed from day one that your group has a slower pace, I would recommend getting up early that morning. Peaking Lafayette is definitely the most difficult part of the hike, with a couple of false peaks and a lot of open rock and exposure. After that, it's a lot of ridge line hiking (and totally beautiful), which means you should be ultra-careful about weather (getting too hot, rain, etc.) Then you'll have a section of pretty flat, woodsy hiking to get to camp -- this is a great time for partner hikes! There's a steep .3 miles downhill to get to camp, so be careful on this, especially if it's wet (and considering your freshmen -- and you! -- will be tired at this point).
     
    For days one and two, your water sources are at camp. Day one should be fine with the water your freshmen will have with them (from getting off the bus); for day two, we filled the collapsible water jug, and both of us (leaders) had an extra water bottle. We drank pretty much all of that by the time we got to camp. Also, for these days, both of the campsites are great -- big group platforms, bear boxes (which is awesome because the trees are not ideal for bear bagging at all), and an outhouse. Keep in mind that unless the TWO program timeline changes, you will be hitting these two sites on Friday and Saturday night. They may be more crowded with weekend through-hikers, so you'll want to try and get there as early as possible. Day 2, we didn't get to camp until 6:30PM, and half our group ended up sleeping in the caretaker's tent, and half on a small group platform. It wasn't a big deal (and the caretaker was super-nice), but if we had gotten there any later, it could have been an issue.
    Day three, you start out with some uphill to Liberty and Flume, and then it's a lot of downhill and flat hiking to get to camp. Remember to fill up at the beginning of the day, because you won't hit water again until you're near Franconia. You actually do about the same distance as day two, but it's WAY less difficult. Still, in some of the downhill sections, be very careful -- there's one area with a lot of ladders/stair to climb down, and if it's wet, you'll want to take your time. When you get to the Franconia area, you'll be walking parallel to the river for a bit, then you'll see a wooden foot bridge to your right. This part gets a little confusing, but here's what we found was the easiest way to cross the river and get to camp:
    Do NOT cross the bridge: instead, when you're facing the bridge, go down to the right of it, and walk along the rocks in that direction along the river for maybe .1 miles (not far at all), until you see that the river on your left is a more calm. Cross the river here (and remind your freshmen how badass they are for doing it), and then head left, hugging the shoreline until you see a pathway/opening on your right (tiny distance, probably less than .1 miles). This is your campsite for nights three and four! You'll see the outhouse, and all the areas to set up camp are to your right. There's a bear box here, too -- a big red one near site 15 (so you technically don't ever need to bear bag on this trip).
    Day four is pure playtime: cross the river the way you did the day before, and you'll see a sign at the wooden footbridge for telling you that Franconia Falls is .4 miles to your right. Have fun! We came back from the falls around 4PM, to have lots of time for pizza-making and group bonding at camp, which worked out well. Also, little tip: there's a rad swimming spot about a quarter mile from camp. Go past the outhouse (the outhouse is on your right) and walk along that pathway, and you'll see where you can duck to the left for a more private, lesser-known swimming area. We went there at the end of day three, after we plopped down our stuff and set up camp. Totally worth it!
    Day five, you're just waking up and doing a mostly flat, 3-mile walk-out. Not hard at all. If you get to the parking lot early, that may be a good time to have a little debrief from your trip with your freshmen. So, in general: it is a very challenging trip toward the beginning, when you have the most weight on you (something to be mindful of, especially in terms of how this can affect group dynamics), but it is incredibly rewarding to end your trip in the Franconia Falls area.

    -AFlo & Weezy

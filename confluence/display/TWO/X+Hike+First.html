    This is a great trip.

    To start, the bus ride is pretty long but provides for a nice time for intra- and inter-group bonding, which was great. We arrived at around 130 and after a quick lunch made for Horns Pond Site. It was not a particularly difficult hike the first day (and was very easy to find - get dropped off at the BIG bus turnaround about 0.5 miles down the Stratton Brook Road then hike approx 100 yards down the RIGHT fork to the AT) but it was pretty long so do your best on cracking the whip because otherwise it'll put you into camp pretty late. Check in with the caretaker at Horns Pond site, ours did a little LNT ethics course and was intense but very helpful. You will most likely NOT get a lean-to, they are reserved for thru-hikers and smaller groups, so be prepared for that. This site was very very serious about LNT stuff so be hyper-aware of that.

     We can't really comment on Day 2 or 3 of the hiking because we skipped it due to weather. We opted to remain at the Horns Pond site for the day, get warm and dry and be ready to make A20 (E.jo and Austin) feel better when they arrived. It was not difficult though to hike down the next day and meet SS at the drop point and have them drive us to the canoeing swap point. This part was a bit confusing so try to stay together and figure out where the best spot to park the vans is (if you have to do this, otherwise look for the site (Round Barn?) located on the peninsula). It may take a little searching but you'll find the other hybrid group if you search a bit. Be sure to discuss a plan of action before though so you both aren't sitting around waiting for the other to find you. I suspect that the canoeing group will arrive first most times (unless it is very windy in the wrong direction) so maybe plan on having the hiking group be the searchers and the canoeists wait.

     Day 3 (Day 1 of canoeing) was very short, especially with a tailwind (you can see Hurricane Island from the beach where we met X2). However, the sites are first-come-first-served and there was a group on the island already (though the site looked really really cool) so we had to continue along the NE shore for about a half mile to the next campsite (marked on the map). It's not the best but it's located on a nice beach which is fun AND hard to miss so don't be too afraid of passing it by. Some rocks made for some hangups but overall it was not bad. Going this extra half mile was great because the next day we woke up to a 180 degree wind shift and increase to 15-20 knots. We had to wait it out at the campsite until about 2 or 3 PM, when we deemed it ok to set out. Paddling in the wind sucks but HUG the coast and it'll be a lot easier. The day wasn't too bad, though we had to do some canoeing after dark (which was actually really really cool, especially since the wind died). The final site was exactly where we expected it to be, located on a nice beach immediately after you turn left around a point on the SE shore. I'd suggest going outside of any islands even if there's wind because they can get really shallow (check out the Google Maps satellite image (just googlemap 45.196261,-70.395119 to see what I mean). The final day was not bad at all since there was no wind, we left at about 7 AM and arrived at 9.59. The pickup is a dirt road coming down through some trees on the left before the lake turns the corner (googlemap 45.144518,-70.434424). There's a little convenience store with cheap stuff if you walk along School St and turn left on 27. The bus driver thought the pickup was at a different spot so I'm not really sure where they were told to go but this spot was the obvious one from the water - maybe adjust the directions given to the driver Ellmore/coordinators? Also, ask the coordinators what to do about the canoes because that was unclear. What if the bus arrives before the canoe guy? Do you just leave them? etc.

    Any questions email me at john.r.hoopes@gmail.com. X1GetSome.

    Cyril J. Fountain says:

    This Trip was amazing. Honestly, I don't think that you can find better scenery...

    This Trip was amazing. Honestly, I don't think that you can find better scenery, better sunset views or a better mix of lakes and mountains on any other wilderness trip.

    So the trip.
    Evangelia and I hiked first and paddled second. The drop off was well marked and visible from the road but its a very small rd and easy to miss if the bus driver is rollin along. I really suggest studying the area around the drop off point well so that you know what you are looking for (landmarks or the names of adjacent roads) and that you can direct the driver if you coming from either direction.

    The first day's hike is longer than expected and doesn't start getting steep until maybe the last 2hours. The trails also aren't blazed very well and are a slightly overgrown in many locations so following the trail isn't always straight forward. The campsite is quite large and you can't miss it. Check in with the caretaker before you set up tarps etc as there is an area for groups and another area for individuals. This wasn't made clear to us when we set up so we ended up setting up in the individual camping area. watch out for chipmunks. They are vicious and prevalent. The water source was gorgeous.

    Day two was long, like really long with freshman. make sure you lots of extra water. There were several marked springs on our maps but unfortunately since there hadn't been much rain they had dried up. The ridges were quite exposed (obviously) and had great views. The first and hardest assent in my opinion wasn't well blazed. stay on the rocks and scramble up the side of the mountain rather than trying to walk around or circumvent the bouldering. be careful on the descents. as the day winds down (long day) the freshman get tired and careless. we had two bad stumbles and one sprained ankle maybe 40mins out of the second day's camp site.

    The campsite was awesome. large spacious. great bear bagging trees and had a good water source.

    Day three was a short day of hiking and then a long day of canoeing. we met up with x2 at the designated campsite and waiting for almost 4 hours for support staff. the camp grounds were so large that support staff, even though they had arrived maybe 20min after both groups had united, we didn't find them literally, for hours. i suggest working out a specific location to meet with SS and X2. the front entrance or a car park or the point. there are lots or recognizable landmarks so pick one make hang out for a bit and wait for everyone else to arrive.

    the whether was really rough (white caps) when we decided to head out the first day. Evangelia and I decided to wait out the roughness and paddle a little bit later. this worked out amazingly well in that our group bonded on the beach, but the paddle was long and we paddled by night. Night paddling wasn't difficult and it was easy enough to navigate, but finding a good canoe landing or a pathway into the island was difficult. our stoves didn't work this night or for the rest of the trip so we cooked over fires.

    day four was a long day of canoeing again and we didn't make camp until just before sunset. however it was perfect timing because we got a gorgeous unobstructed view of the sunset. again we cooked over an open fire. there didn't seem to be any specific tenting/tarping areas so we found comfy spots and settled down for the night.

    The last day of canoeing was fast and we got to pickup point as the bus showed up. the canoe rental people were there as well so there were no issues with leaving the canoes unattended.

    great trip. spectacular co-leader. do it. get some.

    if you have more questions link me at cjf8790@gmail.com

    Just to add, about bear bagging on the first two days. Night 1 is just below the tree line and fairly exposed, and the campsite provides plastic containers for food, so don't bother trying to hang a bear bag. The 2nd day's campsite had A LOT of bear warnings, more than a typical Maine camping area. I am not sure if this is a regular thing due to it being such a sheltered area, and will be the case this year, or if the bear population moves around. Like Duke said, the bear bagging trees are great! Take advantage and make them far far away from your campsite. You usually spend this night with another Maine trip going in the opposite direction. The two trips all bear bagged together and it was a great time that got everyone practicing.

    Also the day 3 canoe length is deceivingly short (as in it may take longer than you think) and how much of a struggle it is depends entirely on the weather and the paddling ability of your group. There is a nice inlet at the crossover point with x2 that provides a safe area to teach paddling skills before shoving off. I believe we docked our canoes on the right far side of Hurricane island. Look for our message in the guestbook!

    X2 (Orli & Sam) Trip Report (Trip X1 - hiking first)

    This is the best route. Seriously. You win. 

    Day 1: Hike the AT from rt. 27 to the Horns

    The dropoff was easy to see from the road, but we missed it at first and had to turn around. When you see a pull off/parking area with a hiker/trailhead sign on the N side of the road, that's probably it!!! The hike to our first campsite at the Horns was not too difficult. It would have been nice to get up there earlier because there are a couple of large Group Sites, but they were already occupied by other college groups. Also, it would have been awesome to swim in the Horns Pond if we'd had time. We checked in with the camp area's caretaker, he said to poke around and see whatever was still open. We took up two smaller sites - sleeping in one and keeping our gear in the other… the campsite caretaker told us in the morning that that was not OK because there were people that came in late looking for a tentsite and our gear was just sitting there. oops!

    Day 2: Hike from the Horns to Safford Brook Campsite

    We should have gotten an earlier start - it was dark by the time we got into our campsite. We also should have encouraged our kids to drink more water and refill before leaving the Horns -- water sources over Avery & West Peak are SCARCE/trickling at best. We took a couple of side hikes that were totally worth it - we hiked up the North Horn - SO pretty!! Maybe that's why we were so late getting into camp…gage the athleticism of your group when choosing to do these extra sections. The trail itself was not very challenging, but everyone in our group was in pretty good shape. Summiting Avery & West was really awesome. The 2000 mile mark on the AT is up there; you'll be coming through with a lot of thru-hikers who are on their way to katahdin, it's pretty cool to get to see them hit this landmark. 

    We tried to fill up our water bottles at a spring between avery & west - THIS SPRING IS DRY and has been for a long time. Do not count on it. It would be great to get water hiked in on this day. 

    Watch out for the signs to the safford brook campsite - we almost missed them. Once you get to the campsite, there's a large group site tucked way in the back. It's the best - plenty of space, and secluded from the other campsites, so you won't be upsetting all the thru hikers if you're chatting past dark. GET THIS CAMPSITE IF YOU CAN! Otherwise, be respectful of other folks who might be trying to sleep nearby :)

    **Bearbagging at both of these campsites - they had bear boxes to use instead. Lots of rodents - keep your stuff in the canisters provided!

     DAY 3: Hike Safford Brook to swapping point, canoe to hurricane island

    Gentle downhill hike, give your group 1.5 - 3 hrs. We aimed to meet up with Ben & Anna at like 11, but it was extremely difficult to find our meeting point. The hut described in old trip plans/marked on the map is very difficult to find. You should plan to meet each other at the group picnic area in the campground. That is easy to locate from both the road and from the lake. This was the single most difficult part of our trip. Sam/Orli/Ben spent ~1.5 hrs running around this whole area looking for each other. Oops. Work this out ahead of time. It really shouldn't be that difficult. We assumed it would be easy to find each other. I wonder what other leaders did in the past and how this was not mentioned to any of us.

    We had lunch and talked about some paddlestrokes before heading over to Hurricane Island. It would be nice to do some capsize drills at the beach before loading up the canoes. The paddle to hurricane was pretty quick. We got there with plenty of sunlight, so we took a quick dip in the lake and made dinner. There are two campsites on the island - we didn't really think one was much "better" than the other. We had the island to ourselves which was really nice, I think a perk The potty was gross/overflowing. ICK. Other than that, this spot was AMAZING. Loons at night. Fog coming down the mountains onto the lake. COULDA BEEN ON THE OCEAN IN ALASKA. It was beautiful. 

     Day 4: Canoe, Hurricane Island to Savage Farm

    We paddled into the wind the whole day, so we swapped up paddling partners throughout the day to pair stronger paddlers with weaker ones, and to give folks a rest who'd been paddling hard. The toughest part was coming around the corner to our campsite… it was very windy. Super cool paddle, you get to see pretty much everything you just hiked. Also, there's some history about the town of flagstaff that was abandoned/torched when plans were made to dam/flood the valley.

    Day 5: Canoe, Savage farm to take-out

    We had no trouble following our map to the take-out. Easy paddle, awesome trip, DO IT.

    2012 Trip Leaders: Brad DeBattista and Eloise Libre

    NH 9
    Trails: The trails were overall very well-marked, except for the Rocky Branch Trail on the last day. When first leaving Rocky Branch Shelter No. 2, you have to cross the river (it had rained the night before, so this was a bit more treacherous, but you'll be fine) and then go LEFT up the trail. It gets a bit confusing when you reach an intersection with another, unlabeled trail at a laminated sign talking about how there's no campfires allowed within 1/4 mile radius of the campsite. Do NOT take this trail off to the left, just keep going up on the trail you were on before. For us, the trail was a bit wet from the rain, and at times we thought we might just be going up a creek, but this turned out to be the right trail after all (although I don't think there were any signs until you reach the trailhead at the end). Other than that, Raymond Path is pretty small and grassy at parts (rull steep when you first get on it), but it was marked well enough for us not to have any problems. Also the Boot Spur connecting trail or whatever is super steep; plan plenty of time for the morning of Day 4.
    Shelters and Campsites: The Osgood Tentsite was very nice, featuring plenty of tent platforms, an outhouse, and a big fire ring area. We met lots of interesting thruhikers here who gave our freshmen a good perspective on what it means to devote such a huge part of your life to the wilderness. We spent nights 2 and 3 at The Hermit Lake Shelters, which we referred to as an outdoor Hilton. They have a bunch of 3- and 4-sided shelters (we'd recommend 3), clean water (that you get to pump yourself), picnic tables right outside each shelter, a drain for wastewater, and well maintained restrooms (not an outhouse). We spent the last night at Rocky Branch Shelter No. 2, which was a bit more run-down shelter (there were holes in the ceiling that we covered with tarps before it rained), and a fire pit. There is also only one of these so you might have to share it if another group shows up like we did.
    Swimming: There wasn't a ton of opportunities to swim, but we planned a lot of time on Day 4 (which we recommend), so we fit some in on the last part of the trail that day (there are some decent spots along the Rocky Branch River; the water will be COLD).
    Water Sources: We never really had to ration water, but definitely be aware of where it is along the trail (it was a ways in on Day 4). Every campsite has a water source at/right next to it, so worst case you'll always have some at the end/beginning of each day. We both carried an extra 1-2.5 liters in camelbacks in our packs, which is a good thing to have if your freshmen need it. If you do that too then you can designate one of your Nalgenes as the sugary KoolAid/Lemonade bottle, which your freshmen will ask you for some of if you talk about how awesome it tastes.
    Difficulty: This is not the easiest trip (you do the tallest mountain in New England), but every day is very doable if you plan accordingly. A lot of our kids said that Day 2 was the hardest (first full day with packs), but the ascent on Day 3 and the morning of Day 4 have quite a bit of elevation too. With that in mind though, we never got to camp after the sun went down, and we had 2 freshmen that struggled a bit and significantly slowed down the group. Just keep the right attitude, take plenty of breaks, and you'll be fine.

    Final Notes: The top of Washington can be quite a let-down since there's a parking lot, a train, restaurants, overweight old tourists, etc. up there, so doing your surprise treat up there might not be the best time. The top of Boott Spur the next day though is much better, especially if you have a gorgeous clear day, and it feels much more like an achievement and real summit. Be careful if there's lightning that day though, there's a good few hours of hiking along the ridge.

    -- Brad & Eloise
